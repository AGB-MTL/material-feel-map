# Sensation Becomes Feeling Becomes Meaning

### An introduction to the Material Feel Map

Some of the senses have precise, shared languages. Color has RGB, hue-saturation-value, Pantone books, and a century of color science behind it. Sound has pitch, timbre, and a notation dense enough to rebuild a symphony from marks on a page. Touch has nothing like this. Ask ten people how a fabric feels and you get ten vocabularies, most of them borrowed from the other senses: warm, soft, rich, clean. There is no reliable way to know whether any two of them mean the same thing. There is no swatch book for the feel of the world.

This matters more than it looks, because touch is where a lot of human judgment actually happens. We decide whether a jar of moisturizer is worth its price in the first second our fingers meet it. We reject a sustainable package because it feels cheap in the hand, whatever the spec sheet says. We trust a tool that feels solid and distrust one that feels hollow, often before we have thought about either. These reactions move markets and sink good products, and they slow the adoption of materials the world needs to switch to. Several fields have studied them: psychophysics, affective neuroscience, and the design world's work on materials experience. But that knowledge sits scattered across disciplines. There is no shared standard for describing what a material means to the hand, and even less for predicting it.

The Material Feel Map tries to pull those scattered threads into one shared vocabulary, and to build it around a specific claim about how touch works. It is a starting point, not a finished thing. Use it, argue with it, improve it.

## The claim

**Sensation becomes feeling becomes meaning.** A material meets the body first as raw sensation: a texture, a temperature, a resistance. Almost at once that sensation turns into a feeling, pleasant or unpleasant, calming or unsettling. Then the feeling settles into a meaning, usually without our noticing. The material comes to signify something. It reads as luxurious, or honest, or clinical, or fake.

**Sensation** is the first of the three layers, and the only one we can already put numbers on. A material begins as a small set of physical qualities the hand registers directly, before any feeling or meaning is attached.

Most of the careful measurement of touch stops here. Psychophysics has done the hard work of establishing the perceptual dimensions of tactile experience: roughness at coarse and fine scales, hardness and compliance, warmth and coolness, the friction and moisture of a surface (Okamoto, Nagano & Yamada, 2013; Picard et al., 2003). It tells us what a material is to the fingertip. It does not tell us what the material does to the person. It is like measuring the wavelength of light without asking what red means. In the empirical work that follows, each of these sensory qualities is tied to an instrument reading, from surface topography to compliance to friction to thermal response, so the vocabulary rests on numbers a materials scientist can reproduce rather than on adjectives alone.

One caveat before moving up the stack: the order is not strictly one-way. What we expect a material to mean can shape what we feel, and culture tunes the whole chain. But as a first orientation, sensation to meaning is the order in which a material reveals itself: first to the hand, then to the mind.

Underneath those dimensions is a stranger story about how the hand reads a surface at all. Coarse and fine textures are not sensed the same way. Broad features register from their spatial layout, and a still finger can feel them. Fine ones appear only when skin slides across the surface and sets up faint vibrations, the old duplex account of texture perception. Those sliding vibrations reach well past the classic touch areas, into auditory cortex among other places (Roberts et al., 2023). Touch is already multisensory well before it becomes meaning. And what reaches awareness is not a clean readout of the skin. In the velvet hand illusion, two smooth wires stroked between the palms produce the vivid feel of velvet that is not physically there, and imaging shows that felt texture arising from a top-down brain network rather than the fingertips alone (Rajaei et al., 2018). That is direct evidence for the point above: meaning and expectation feed back to shape what we feel.

**Feeling** is the second layer, and it has started to get real attention. Much of it centers on the nerve fibers that respond to slow, gentle, skin-temperature touch and seem tuned for emotional and social signaling rather than for fine discrimination (Löken et al., 2009; McGlone, Wessberg & Olausson, 2014). Pleasantness, on this view, is not a coat of paint over sensation. It behaves more like a sense of its own.

**Meaning** is the third layer, and the least charted, though not untouched. Designers have taken it seriously. The materials experience framework of Karana, Pedgley, and Rognoli separates the sensorial, affective, and interpretive sides of how we live with materials: how we sense them, how they move us, and what they come to signify (Karana, Pedgley & Rognoli, 2014; Giaccardi & Karana, 2015). Tools like meaning-driven materials selection and expressive-sensorial atlases have charted parts of it (Karana, Hekkert & Kandachar, 2010; Rognoli, 2010). What is still missing is a shared, standardized coordinate system: an agreed vocabulary for where luxury, authenticity, trust, and cheapness sit, stable enough that different studies, materials, and people can be compared. This is where the stakes are highest and the standardization is weakest. The map's meaning layer is an opinionated first proposal for what belongs there. It does not claim to discover new territory. It tries to give the known territory common coordinates.

## How to read the map

Touch is not one quality but several. A surface has a texture (rough or smooth), a give (hard or soft), a temperature (cool or warm), a moisture (dry or moist), a grip (slippery or grippy), and a weight (light or substantial). These vary on their own, and that independence is the thing the map is built to respect. Silk is soft and cool. Wool is soft and warm. A chilled gel pack is soft, cool, and moist. Softness tells you nothing about warmth, and no honest map should force the two to move together.

So the map does not sort touch into fixed types. You set each dimension yourself, and the combination is what carries a feeling and a meaning. Soft and warm read as comforting, and then as nurturing. Change one dimension, though, and the meaning moves with it: soft and cool read as calm and refined instead. Meaning lives in the combination, not in any single sensation, and you can watch it shift the moment you change one setting.

A word about the status of these mappings, because it matters more than anything else here: they are **hypotheses, not laws**. The map suggests that soft-and-warm tends to feel comforting. Sometimes that holds firmly. Sometimes it breaks. A few combinations are contested on purpose. Rough and dry can read as natural, artisanal, honest, the texture of unbleached linen or a hand-thrown cup. The same surface can also read as unfinished and cheap. Which reading wins depends on the material, the person, and the culture around them. That ambiguity is the most interesting thing the map turns up, not a defect in it, and pinning it down, knowing when a combination carries which meaning and for whom, is the research this project exists to do. Plenty of combinations have no settled reading at all. The map leaves those blank on purpose, because the blanks are where the work is.

## Why a shared vocabulary, and why now

The goal is to establish a shared ontology of material feel: a common, named set of sensory dimensions and the meanings they carry, so that people and machines can work from the same coordinates instead of inventing a private vocabulary every time. The idea has a lineage. The most useful instruments in the sensory sciences have often been shared vocabularies. The Wine Aroma Wheel (Noble et al., 1987) gave an entire industry a common language for something that had lived only in private experience, and it has anchored decades of research and practice since. The Coffee Taster's Flavor Wheel (Specialty Coffee Association, 2016) did the same for coffee. Neither worked because it was complete or final. They worked because they were shared. Once a field agrees on a vocabulary, observations can be compared, pooled, and built on.

Those are literally wheels, and this project started as one. But a wheel cut into wedges forces everything in a wedge to travel together. It welds soft to warm as if they were one thing, which is the confusion an honest account of touch has to avoid. So this is a map, not a wheel: independent dimensions that combine, keeping the vocabulary shared without letting the shape smuggle in claims it hasn't earned. The aim is the same. The shape is only more careful about what it claims.

Touch is overdue for a shared vocabulary, and the timing is not an accident. Two things make the meaning of materials newly practical to worry about.

The first is the materials transition. Regulation, climate targets, and corporate commitments are pushing the world to replace plastics, virgin materials, and animal-derived materials at scale. Many of the substitutes are technically excellent and fail anyway, in the hand, on feel. Consumer acceptance of the whole shift is bottlenecked on a variable that is hard to measure at scale and harder to design toward. A rigorous account of material meaning is not a nicety for that transition. It may be a precondition.

The second is artificial intelligence. We are building machines that model the physical world, and they learn it almost entirely through images and text. They can describe how a material looks and recite what it is made of, but they carry no grounded sense of how it feels to a person. Recent work finds that when these models do reason about touch, they diverge from us in systematic, material-specific ways (Zhong et al., 2026). That gap will not close by scraping more text or rendering more images. What things feel like to people has to be measured from people. That measurement is the long arc of this work.

## What comes next

This is an early version, a starting point rather than a conclusion. It is open so people can use it, push on it, and correct it, and so the vocabulary stops being mine and starts being shared.

From here the work turns empirical. The map's combinations become questions to test with real people touching real materials, using the map itself to record what they report. Pair those responses with careful physical measurement of the same materials and you have ground truth for something bigger: a benchmark for how well any system, human or machine, can predict the felt meaning of a material from its physical properties. Whether the six dimensions are really independent or partly collapse into each other, and how best to represent them for a model to learn, is one of the questions the data will settle rather than something the map assumes. The dimensions are a human-readable starting point; the underlying taxonomy ships as structured data so it can be treated as one. The far goal is a model of felt experience precise enough to run backward, to design a material toward a target feeling instead of only describing the feeling of one that already exists.

It is a long road, and this is the first marker on it. The thesis is already visible in the map. Set a few sensations, watch the meaning they add up to, and you have sensation becoming feeling becoming meaning. The rest is the slow work of proving, combination by combination and material by material, exactly how.

If any of this lands for you, whether you study touch, design with materials, build models of the world, or just notice how much the things in your hands shape how you feel, I would be glad to have you use the map, disagree with it, and help make it better.
